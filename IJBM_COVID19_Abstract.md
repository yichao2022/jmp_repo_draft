# Title: The Epidemiological Cost of Behavioral Delay: Integrating Intertemporal Preferences into SEIR Dynamics for COVID-19 Vaccination Policy

## Authors
**Yichao Jin**, Ph.D. Candidate, The University of Texas at Dallas  

---

**Background:** 
While ultimate vaccination coverage is a standard metric for public health success during the COVID-19 pandemic, the *timing* of uptake—frequently hindered by administrative wait times and policy frictions—critically determines outbreak trajectories. Traditional epidemiological models typically treat vaccination rates as supply-driven constants, neglecting the structural time preferences, psychological frictions, and behavioral decision-making of the population. Understanding these behavioral insights is crucial for evaluating the true efficacy of the COVID-19 response and preparing for future public health crises.

**Methods:** 
We develop a behaviorally-augmented SEIR model that endogenizes vaccination uptake as a function of individual delay aversion ($\kappa$). We recover structural discounting parameters from a large-scale discrete choice experiment ($N=1,027$) conducted in Wuhan, China. These parameters are integrated into transmission dynamics to explicitly quantify the "behavioral gap" between vaccine supply capacity and actual uptake, isolating the epidemiological costs of procrastination driven by policy-induced wait times.

**Results:** 
Our empirical results identify significant present-biased preferences ($\hat{\kappa}=0.225$) in the population, highlighting a steep psychological cost of waiting. Simulation results demonstrate that this behavioral delay aversion can lead to a severe "behavioral surge" in COVID-19 infections. Compared to a purely supply-constrained scenario (Attack Rate $\approx$ 22.5\%), the introduction of behavioral delay and policy friction leads to a dramatic expansion of the outbreak (Attack Rate $\approx$ 63.6\%), representing a 183\% increase in cumulative infections. Subgroup analysis reveals that older adults ($\hat{\kappa}=0.327$) and those with neutral institutional trust ($\hat{\kappa}=0.356$) exhibit the highest behavioral hurdles, translating to the greatest biological vulnerability during the pandemic.

**Conclusion:** 
Behavioral discounting and policy-induced wait times acted as structural drivers of epidemic risk during the COVID-19 pandemic. Our behavioral insights reveal that public health interventions that solely expand supply, without actively addressing the "psychological cost of waiting" and mitigating policy frictions, consistently underperform. Future pandemic responses must prioritize behavioral "de-biasing" and administrative streamlining to bridge the critical gap between vaccine availability and timely biological protection.

**Keywords:** Behavioral Medicine; COVID-19 Response; Policy Friction; Intertemporal Choice; Delay Aversion; Behavioral SEIR; Vaccination Timing.
