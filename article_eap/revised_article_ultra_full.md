## Abstract
**Background:** Vaccination timing critically determines the duration for which individuals remain biologically exposed to infectious risk. While aggregate coverage is a common metric, the intertemporal aspect of uptake is often overlooked in policy evaluation.
**Methods:** This study quantifies intertemporal preferences for vaccination using a discrete choice experiment among 1,027 adults in Wuhan, China. Waiting time (0–6 months) is embedded directly into vaccine attributes alongside efficacy, side effects, origin, and cash incentives. We estimate time-discount parameters using mixed logit models and calculate marginal willingness-to-accept (MWTA) for delayed protection.
**Results:** Findings reveal pronounced present-oriented preferences: respondents strongly discount delayed vaccination, with a hyperbolic parameter $\kappa = 0.225$ and an exponential parameter $\delta = 0.160$. Delay aversion is highly heterogeneous. Individuals with chronic medical conditions, lower institutional trust, and irregular preventive-health routines exhibit substantially steeper discounting and require greater compensation to tolerate waiting.
**Conclusions:** Vaccination delays impose regressive behavioural burdens, disproportionately affecting medically and socially vulnerable populations. By linking time preferences to biological risk and institutional context, this study advances the understanding of inequalities in preventive health timing. We highlight the importance of incorporating temporal behavior and the "behavioral time tax" into public health delivery strategies.

**Keywords:** Vaccination timing; Intertemporal preferences; Health capital; Biological vulnerability; Institutional trust; Delay aversion; Discrete choice experiment.

## 1. Introduction
Timely access to biological protection is a central determinant of human exposure to infectious disease and the preservation of health capital (Castioni et al., 2024; Shankar et al., 2024). While vaccination coverage is often treated as a binary outcome—vaccinated or not—the timing of vaccination critically shapes the duration for which individuals remain unprotected and biologically vulnerable. Even modest delays can extend periods of exposure and shift outbreak dynamics, making the intertemporal timing of uptake as critical as aggregate population coverage (Rasmussen et al., 2021). Delays in vaccination therefore prolong exposure to infection risk, during which individuals face potential health deterioration and uncertainty about future protection. Understanding how humans evaluate such delays is essential for explaining observed patterns of preventive health behaviour under infectious risk.

From an intertemporal perspective, vaccination timing represents a health investment decision in which immediate costs—such as effort, inconvenience, or perceived side effects—are traded off against delayed and uncertain biological benefits (Frederick et al., 2002). When protection is postponed, individuals must decide whether the future reduction in infection risk sufficiently compensates for remaining exposed in the present. These trade-offs are not uniform across populations. Institutional trust (Krastev et al., 2023), perceived biological vulnerability (Bok et al., 2024), and stable preventive-health routines (Kumar et al., 2025) shape how future protection is perceived and discounted, giving rise to systematic heterogeneity in tolerance for delayed protection. These determinants appear across diverse institutional settings, reflecting general features of intertemporal health decision-making.

A growing body of research documents that vaccination timing is influenced by behavioural factors such as impatience, risk perception, and institutional trust, alongside logistical access constraints (Anderson et al., 2023; Robertson et al., 2024). However, much of this literature continues to treat waiting time as an administrative friction rather than as a biologically meaningful interval of continued exposure (Lievre et al., 2024; Tran et al., 2025). From a physiological perspective, delay is not neutral: each additional period without protection entails ongoing exposure during which infection risk, potential morbidity, and uncertainty accumulate. Despite this, few discrete choice experiments have directly quantified how individuals value delayed protection as an intertemporal health trade-off (Yue et al., 2021; Gong et al., 2020), and existing studies of health-related discounting have not linked sensitivity to delay to structural determinants such as institutional trust or chronic medical vulnerability (van der Pol & Cairns, 2001). Without explicitly measuring the behavioural cost of remaining unprotected, it remains difficult to assess how delays in access translate into unequal health burdens.

### 1.1. Contributions of the Study
This study addresses these gaps by examining how individuals value the *timing* of vaccination as an intertemporal health investment. We use a discrete choice experiment (DCE) administered to 1,027 adults in Wuhan, China. Wuhan provides a particularly informative empirical setting; as a city with high population density and early pandemic exposure, it constitutes a high-salience environment where biological consequences of delay are readily understood. This study makes three specific contributions: (1) it provides a DCE-based quantification of intertemporal preferences for vaccination; (2) it demonstrates that sensitivity to delay is systematically patterned by biological and behavioral characteristics; and (3) it reveals that identical waiting periods impose unequal experiential burdens across the population, contributing to inequalities in health protection.

## 2. Theoretical Framework and Economic Rationale

### 2.1. Vaccination as Health Capital Investment
Following Grossman’s (1972) foundational framework, vaccination is an investment in health capital. Individuals weigh immediate utility costs—uptake effort, anxiety, or logistical frictions—against the discounted preservation of future health stocks. Vaccination decisions are inherently intertemporal because postponing protection extends the period of "unprotected exposure" to infectious risk.

### 2.2. Discounting and Present Bias
The discount parameters $\kappa$ (hyperbolic) and $\delta$ (exponential) summarize these trade-offs. While exponential discounting assumes a constant rate of devaluation over time, the quasi-hyperbolic specification allows for steeper discounting over short horizons. This captures "present bias," where immediacy is disproportionately valued. In the context of vaccination, high discounting implies that short delays impose high behavioral disutility, leading to underinvestment in timely health protection.

### 2.3. Subjective Waiting Time and the Behavioral Delay Multiplier (BDM)
To bridge the gap between objective calendar time and individual experience, we introduce the *Behavioral Delay Multiplier (BDM)*. It is defined as the ratio between perceived and objective waiting time. This multiplier provides a metric for the "psychological time tax" borne by vulnerable groups, where objective delays are experienced as significantly more burdensome.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1. Discrete Choice Experiment Design
We conducted a DCE among 1,027 adults in Wuhan in early 2025. A "COVID-like disease" (CLD) framing was used to elicit structural preferences for a hypothetical respiratory infection with severity comparable to COVID-19. This approach avoids anchoring responses to specific variants or wave-specific emotions, enhancing the generalizability of the recovered parameters. Vaccine alternatives were defined by:
1.  **Vaccination Delay:** 0, 1, 3, or 6 months.
2.  **Vaccine Efficacy:** 50%, 70%, 90%, 95%.
3.  **Side Effects:** Mild, Moderate, Severe.
4.  **Vaccine Origin:** Domestic vs. Imported.
5.  **Cash Incentives:** 0 to 1000 RMB.

### 3.2. Sampling and Data Quality
The sample was stratified by age, gender, and district to match Wuhan's adult population. We implemented rigorous data-quality checks, including dominance tests and attribute non-attendance (ANA) analysis. The dominance violation rate was low (1.8%), and ANA for vaccine delay was 0%, confirming high respondent engagement.

### 3.3. Statistical Analysis
We utilized mixed logit models to recover the distribution of preferences. These models account for random taste variation and unobserved heterogeneity. The estimated coefficients were then used to fit the hyperbolic and exponential discount curves via nonlinear least squares. Marginal willingness-to-accept (MWTA) was computed as the ratio of the delay coefficient to the incentive coefficient.

### 4.2. Quantifying Discounting and Impatience
Fitting the models to time preferences yielded an exponential discount factor $\delta = 0.160$ and a hyperbolic parameter $\kappa = 0.225$. The hyperbolic model captures a sharp initial decline in the value of protection, reflecting strong premiums on immediacy.

### 4.3. Social and Biological Patterning of Delay Aversion
Subgroup analysis (Table 2) reveals that time preferences are not uniform individual traits but are conditioned by risk and context.
*   **Biological Risk:** Respondents with chronic medical conditions exhibit steeper discounting ($\kappa = 0.227$) than those without ($\kappa = 0.183$).
*   **Institutional Trust:** Trust acts as intertemporal capital. High-trust individuals are more patient, while those with lower trust discount future protection more heavily.
*   **Age and Education:** Steeper discounting is observed among older adults (65+) and those with lower educational attainment.

### 4.4. Marginal Willingness-to-Accept (MWTA)
The average respondent requires 52.1 RMB per month to tolerate delay. This burden is highly regressive: low-trust individuals demand 81.3 RMB per month—a 2.4-fold difference compared to high-trust groups (34.2 RMB).

## 5. Discussion: Policy Trade-offs and the "Time Tax"

### 5.1. Biological Vulnerability as a Driver of Timeliness
Heterogeneity in timing preferences reflects adaptive responses to biological risk. For individuals with chronic conditions, remaining unprotected is an amplified risk. Steeper discounting in these groups is a rational response to perceived susceptibility.

### 5.2. Inequality and the Regressive Time Tax
Waiting functions as a regressive "behavioral time tax." Identical delays generate different consequences across groups, extracting the highest psychological cost from the most vulnerable and skeptical. Uniform rollout schedules risk concentrating the costs of waiting where they are hardest to bear.

### 5.3. Policy Recommendations
1.  **Priority Delivery for High-MWTA Groups:** Logistical features like mobile units or priority booking for medically vulnerable populations can maximize uptake more efficiently than financial rewards.
2.  **Trust as Capital:** Transparent communication reduces uncertainty about future protection, effectively "buying" more patience from the population and reducing the behavioral cost of delay.
3.  **Cost-Effectiveness of Timeliness:** For the average respondent, reducing delay by one month is equivalent to providing 50 RMB in incentives. Targeted timeliness is often cheaper and more effective than fiscal transfers.

---

## 6. Conclusion
This study demonstrates that vaccination delay is a biologically consequential and economically significant dimension of health behavior. By quantifying the heterogeneous cost of waiting, we provide a framework for designing public health systems that account for the intertemporal preferences of vulnerable populations.

## Appendices

### Appendix A: Validity Diagnostics
We assessed internal validity through dominance behavior and attribute non-attendance (ANA). Only 1.8% of respondents selected dominated alternatives. ANA for core attributes was zero, confirming that respondents engaged with the full attribute structure of the DCE.

### Appendix B: Sample Representativeness
Comparison with the 2020 Wuhan Census shows high alignment across gender, age, and urban-rural residence. A slight upward shift in college-educated respondents is noted, consistent with online panels.

### Appendix C: Formal Derivations
The discount parameters were obtained by minimizing the sum of squared errors between the observed discount factor $D_{\text{obs}}(t) = \exp(-\beta_{\text{delay}} t)$ and theoretical functions. The Behavioral Delay Multiplier (BDM) is calculated as $1 / (1 - \kappa)$, rescaling the hyperbolic parameter into a measure of perceived inflation of waiting time.
