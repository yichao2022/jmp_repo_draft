# The Economic Cost of Delayed Protection: Intertemporal Preferences and Policy Trade-offs in Vaccination Timing

**Authors:** Yichao Jin, Dohyeong Kim

## Abstract
Timely vaccination is a critical determinant of how long individuals remain biologically exposed to infectious risk. This study employs a discrete choice experiment (DCE) among 1,027 adults in Wuhan, China, to quantify the economic value of vaccination timing. By embedding waiting time directly into vaccine attributes, we estimate time-discount parameters, marginal willingness-to-accept (MWTA) for delay, and a Behavioral Delay Multiplier (BDM) to capture subjective temporal burdens. Our results reveal pronounced short-run impatience, with hyperbolic ($\kappa = 0.225$) and exponential ($\delta = 0.160$) discounting. Delay aversion is systematically patterned: individuals with chronic medical conditions, lower institutional trust, and irregular preventive-health routines exhibit significantly steeper discounting and higher MWTA. We demonstrate that vaccination delays function as a regressive "time tax," disproportionately burdening medically and socially vulnerable populations. These findings provide a framework for evaluating policy trade-offs between logistical convenience improvements and fiscal incentives, offering insights for designing more efficient and equitable public health delivery systems.

**Keywords:** Vaccination timing; Intertemporal preferences; Marginal willingness-to-accept; Policy trade-offs; Biological vulnerability; Institutional trust; Discrete choice experiment.

## 1. Introduction
The global effort to combat infectious diseases often focuses on aggregate vaccination coverage. However, the timing of uptake is equally critical, as it determines the duration of an individual's biological exposure and shaped outbreak dynamics (Castioni et al., 2024). Delays in protection are not merely administrative frictions; they represent periods of ongoing vulnerability during which infection risk, morbidity, and psychological uncertainty accumulate. Understanding how individuals value the immediacy of protection is essential for designing effective and equitable public health policies.

From an economic perspective, vaccination timing involves intertemporal trade-offs where immediate utility costs (effort, side-effect concerns) are weighed against delayed biological benefits (Frederick et al., 2002). These trade-offs are conditioned by the individual's biological risk and institutional environment. While behavioural factors such as impatience and trust are known to influence health behavior (Anderson et al., 2023), few studies have quantified the behavioral cost of delay as a biologically meaningful interval of continued exposure.

This study addresses this gap by using a discrete choice experiment (DCE) in Wuhan, China—a high-salience environment for infectious risk—to recover formal time-preference parameters and estimate the monetary equivalents of vaccination delay. We introduce two key metrics for policy analysis: Marginal Willingness-to-Accept (MWTA) for delay and the Behavioral Delay Multiplier (BDM), which measures the inflation of subjective waiting time.

## 2. Theoretical Framework and Policy Relevance

### 2.1. Vaccination as Intertemporal Health Investment
Following Grossman’s (1972) health-capital framework, vaccination is an investment where immediate costs are traded for the preservation of future health stocks. Postponing this investment extends the period of "unprotected exposure." The discount parameters recovered in this study ($\kappa$ and $\delta$) represent the rate at which individuals trade off present versus future health returns. High discounting implies that even short delays impose substantial behavioral disutility.

### 2.2. The Behavioral Delay Multiplier (BDM)
To capture the experiential burden of waiting, we derive the BDM—the ratio between perceived and objective waiting time. This metric represents a "behavioral time tax" associated with remaining unprotected. In policy terms, the BDM identifies populations for whom objective waiting times are psychologically magnified, leading to higher barriers to uptake.

## 3. Methods

### 3.1. Discrete Choice Experiment Design
We conducted a DCE among 1,027 adults in Wuhan, China, in early 2025. Respondents evaluated vaccine profiles defined by:
1. Vaccination delay (0–6 months)
2. Vaccine efficacy
3. Side effects
4. Vaccine origin (domestic vs. imported)
5. Cash incentives (RMB)

A "COVID-like disease" (CLD) framing was used to elicit structural preferences without anchoring responses to specific historical waves or variants.

### 3.2. Statistical Modeling
We estimated mixed logit models to account for preference heterogeneity. The coefficients were then mapped to exponential ($\delta$) and hyperbolic ($\kappa$) discount parameters. MWTA was calculated as the marginal rate of substitution between vaccination delay and cash incentives.

## 4. Results

### 4.1. Main Model Estimates and Discounting
As shown in Table 1, vaccination delay significantly reduces uptake probability. Standardizing the coefficients reveals that a one-standard-deviation increase in cash incentives nearly offsets the disutility of a corresponding increase in delay.

The hyperbolic model ($\kappa = 0.225$) exhibits a sharp initial decline compared to the exponential model ($\delta = 0.160$), indicating that respondents place a disproportionate premium on immediacy.

### 4.2. Heterogeneity in Delay Aversion
Subgroup analyses reveal pronounced social and biological gradients in time preferences:
- **Biological Vulnerability**: Respondents with chronic medical conditions exhibit steeper discounting, reflecting the higher expected biological cost of remaining unprotected for high-risk individuals.
- **Institutional Trust**: Lower trust in government institutions is associated with greater impatience, suggesting that trust stabilizes expectations about future health returns.
- **Health Routines**: Individuals with irregular preventive-health routines display stronger present bias.

## 5. Discussion: Policy Trade-offs and the "Time Tax"

### 5.1. Evaluating Time-Money Trade-offs
Our MWTA estimates provide a practical lens for policy design. The average respondent requires approximately 52.1 RMB per month to tolerate delay. However, for low-trust individuals, this figure rises to 81.3 RMB—a 2.4-fold difference compared to high-trust groups (34.2 RMB).

### 5.2. Logistical Improvements vs. Financial Incentives
For the average individual, reducing vaccination delay by one month provides the same behavioral gain as a 50 RMB incentive. Since shortening queues or extending clinic hours is often operationally cheaper than large-scale cash transfers, targeting timeliness may offer a higher return on investment for public health budgets.

### 5.3. Policy Recommendations
1. **Priority Access for High-MWTA Groups**: Medically vulnerable and low-trust populations should be prioritized for rapid access (e.g., mobile units, priority booking) to reduce the experiential burden of delay.
2. **Predictable Scheduling**: For populations with stable health routines and higher trust, transparent and reliable scheduling may be sufficient to maintain high uptake without the need for financial rewards.
3. **Integrated Incentives**: Where capacity is constrained, modest immediate rewards may offset the disutility of waiting more effectively for delay-sensitive groups than larger, delayed bonuses.

## 6. Conclusion
This study demonstrates that vaccination delay is a biologically consequential and economically significant dimension of health behavior. By quantifying the heterogeneous cost of waiting, we provide a framework for designing delivery systems that account for the intertemporal preferences of vulnerable populations.
