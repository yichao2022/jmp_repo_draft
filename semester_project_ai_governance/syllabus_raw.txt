Course Description
This graduate seminar provides a comprehensive survey of classical and contemporary social and
economic theories, with a particular focus on the subﬁeld of economic sociology. Students will
explore the foundational works of Marx, Durkheim, and Weber, alongside contemporary
perspectives on networks, social capital, and the "embeddedness" of economic action. Unlike

standard surveys, however, this course treats the economy as an integrated subsystem of the larger
social structure, using the current historical moment as a primary site for theoretical application.
The curriculum is organized around the systemic interplay between economic processes and social
order. We will examine how abstract mechanisms—such as those Simmel analyzed in his
“philosophy of money” and F.A. Hayek’s price signals—coordinate modern life, while utilizing
macrosociological frameworks as a synthetic tools to manage these diverse perspectives.
By grounding core theories in contemporary challenges—including wealth inequality, shifting
political orders, and the social impact of AI—the course demonstrates how classical insights
remain essential for modern policy research, administration, and applied sociology. Students will
develop a sophisticated theoretical toolkit, enabling them to analyze the economy not in isolation,
but as a dynamic engine inextricably linked to the legitimacy, integration, and cultural values of
American society.

Student learning objectives and outcomes
1. Develop a knowledge base of classical and contemporary social and economic theories
2. Develop an understanding of the role theory plays in conducting social science research
3. Apply social-economic theories to a range of contemporary social science research
topics/questions

Note on the Readings
The reading schedule is designed to keep overall workload relatively consistent across weeks, but
reading demands will vary. Some weeks will be lighter, others heavier. This variation reﬂects the
sequential and thematic structure of the course, as di erent phases require di erent types and
lengths of readings.
Readings also di er in di iculty. Some authors are highly philosophical or metaphysical, others are
technically abstract or mathematically oriented, and still others require shifting between macrolevel social theory and individual-level analysis. As a result, some weeks will feel more accessible,
while others will be more demanding.
The assignment schedule includes built-in ﬂexibility to help accommodate these variations.
It is also worth noting that, in addition to their analytical value, the readings have an important
historical and cultural dimension. Many are central texts in the Western intellectual, philosophical,
and social scientiﬁc canon. Regardless of one’s views of ﬁgures such as Marx, Smith, or others
covered in the course, their ideas have had a profound inﬂuence not only on social and economic
thought, but on the structure and functioning of social and economic reality itself.
“Practical men who believe themselves to be quite exempt from any intellectual inﬂuence, are
usually the slaves of some defunct economist. Madmen in authority, who hear voices in the air, are
distilling their frenzy from some academic scribbler of a few years back”
― John Maynard Keynes
In this sense, many of the readings serve the additional purpose of ensuring students are exposed
to key foundational ideas and texts.

Course Requirements
This course is conducted as a colloquium for advanced graduate students. Class sessions take the
form of roundtable discussion, with the instructor serving as a senior facilitator and moderator
rather than a traditional lecturer. The emphasis is on synthesizing and applying core texts to
concrete problems. When appropriate, the instructor will provide theoretical sca olding to support
deeper analytical rigor, including contextualizing readings within broader intellectual traditions
(e.g., German Idealism, ontology, philosophy of science, or major schools of economic thought).
The primary goal is for the class collectively, and students individually, to develop deeper
understanding, insight, and practical application of theoretical frameworks to both enduring and
novel real-world problems.

Theoretical Foundations and Applications Project
The primary course assignment is a semester-long project developed in stages and culminating in a
ﬁnal paper of approximately 15–20 double-spaced pages. Each student will select a topic of
interest and conduct a theoretically grounded analysis using one or more frameworks from the
course.
Students are encouraged to choose topics aligned with their academic interests, research agendas,
or professional goals. Projects may focus on applied issues (e.g., health care, scientiﬁc research,
technology, AI ethics and governance) or on more abstract theoretical questions (e.g., the
normative core of a particular economic theory or practice). Regardless of topic, projects must
examine the interplay between social and economic processes and explicitly apply at least one
course theory.
Students will discuss their proposed projects with the instructor and receive feedback and approval
before proceeding.
Projects will be developed and graded in four phases:


Phase 1: Problem formulation and justiﬁcation (approximately 3 pages)



Phase 2: Analysis of the problem using one theory or major course theme (approximately 3
pages; may include empirical material)



Phase 3: Further analysis synthesizing or comparing two or more theories or themes, with
empirical support—either for theoretical justiﬁcation (e.g., why applying the theory is helpful
given empirical patterns) or veriﬁcation purposes (e.g., why a theory is not borne out by the
empirical facts).



Phase 4: Final paper submission

Additional Course Information
Class Conduct
You are expected to treat everyone in the class with respect at all times. Topics discussed in class
are inherently contentious for deep philosophical and political reasons. It is no accident that the
course readings include ﬁgures that in some ways are considered polar opposites on some
political/philosophical dimensions (e.g., Marx and Hayek) that many people heavily identify with.
We are here to learn and broaden our perspectives by honestly and openly engaging with authors
and fellow students who might have views that are sometimes radically di erent from our own or at
least seem to be so.
