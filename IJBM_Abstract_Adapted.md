# How Humans Value Delayed Protection: Biological Vulnerability and Intertemporal Health Decisions

**Authors**: Yichao Jin and Dohyeong Kim

## Abstract (Adapted for IJBM Special Collection: "Behavioral Medicine Response to COVID-19")

Vaccination timing critically determines how long individuals remain biologically exposed to infectious risk, a key dimension of the behavioral medicine response to COVID-19. This study quantifies intertemporal preferences for vaccination using a discrete choice experiment among 1,027 adults in Wuhan, China, capturing public decision-making during a period of profound uncertainty. By embedding waiting time directly into vaccine attributes, we estimate time-discount parameters and marginal willingness-to-accept (MWTA) for delayed protection. Results reveal pronounced present-oriented behavioral preferences: respondents strongly discount delayed vaccination, exhibiting steep hyperbolic (κ = 0.225) and exponential (δ = 0.160) discounting. Crucially, delay aversion is highly heterogeneous, shaped by both biological vulnerability and policy friction. Individuals with chronic medical conditions, lower institutional trust, and irregular preventive-health routines demonstrate exacerbated discounting, requiring significantly greater compensation to tolerate waiting. These behavioral insights indicate that administrative delays and policy frictions in vaccine rollout impose regressive psychological and behavioral burdens, disproportionately affecting medically and socially vulnerable populations. By linking intertemporal time preferences to biological risk and institutional context, this study provides actionable insights for behavioral medicine interventions, highlighting the urgent need to mitigate policy frictions and incorporate temporal behavior into future pandemic response strategies.

**Keywords**: Vaccination timing; Intertemporal preferences; Behavioral medicine; COVID-19 response; Policy friction; Biological vulnerability; Institutional trust; Discrete choice experiment
