\subsection{Quantifying Structural Delay Aversion ($\kappa$)}

To move beyond qualitative preferences, we recovered the structural delay-aversion parameter ($\kappa$) across the population and specific socio-demographic subgroups. The parameter $\kappa$ represents the population's present-biased resistance to waiting for biological protection. For the overall sample, we estimated a baseline structural discount rate of $\hat{\kappa} = 0.225$ ($p < 0.01$). This value indicates that individuals perceive a significant behavioral "tax" on delayed vaccination, which must be overcome by either clinical superiority or external incentives.

Substantial heterogeneity was observed when partitioning the sample by institutional trust, health status, and socioeconomic factors (see Table \ref{tab:subgroup_kappa}). Crucially, we found that populations with \textit{neutral institutional trust} exhibit the highest behavioral hurdle ($\hat{\kappa} = 0.356$), followed by those with a \textit{primary education} ($\hat{\kappa} = 0.348$). 

Notably, we also identified elevated delay sensitivity among biologically and psychologically vulnerable groups. Individuals reporting a \textit{chronic medical condition} displayed a higher $\kappa$ of $0.288$, reflecting an acute valuation of timely protection under heightened health risk. Furthermore, those with a \textit{prior adverse vaccine reaction} exhibited a structural hurdle of $\hat{\kappa} = 0.315$, suggesting that negative past experiences function as a behavioral anchor that inflates the perceived cost of waiting. In contrast, \textit{rural residents} demonstrated the lowest delay sensitivity ($\hat{\kappa} = 0.174$), suggesting a higher tolerance for administrative waiting times in these settings.
