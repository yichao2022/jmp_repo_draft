# Title: How Humans Value Delayed Protection: Behavioral Insights into Vaccine Hesitancy and Policy Friction During COVID-19

**Background:** The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted that vaccination timing critically determines the duration of biological exposure to infectious risk, yet many individuals delay immunization even when vaccines are free and accessible. While traditional models focus on binary uptake, this study quantifies the behavioral cost of waiting as an intertemporal health investment, emphasizing how policy frictions and waiting periods exacerbate delay aversion.

**Methods:** Using a large-scale discrete choice experiment ($N=1,027$) conducted in Wuhan, China, during the COVID-19 recovery phase, we recover structural time-preference parameters characterizing sensitivity to delayed biological protection. Mixed Logit and Conditional Logit models were utilized to identify individual-level taste heterogeneity and stable population-level Marginal Willingness-to-Accept (MWTA).

**Results:** The results reveal pronounced present-oriented preferences: respondents strongly discount delayed vaccination, yielding a hyperbolic parameter $\kappa = 0.225$ and an exponential parameter $\delta = 0.160$. These parameters translate into an average MWTA of approximately 47 to 52 RMB per month of waiting. Furthermore, delay aversion is systematically patterned by biological and social vulnerability. Specifically, individuals with lower institutional trust or chronic medical conditions exhibit substantially steeper discounting, resulting in a significantly higher psychological "time tax" (e.g., MWTA of 81.3 RMB for low-trust vs. 34.2 RMB for high-trust individuals).

**Conclusions:** By linking behavioral parameters to biological risk and policy-induced waiting times, we show that vaccination delays impose regressive behavioral burdens. A "Behavioral Delay Multiplier" reveals that subjective inflation of waiting time can substantially increase vulnerability. These findings offer actionable behavioral medicine insights for designing time-consistent public health strategies that minimize policy friction, reduce the psychological cost of waiting, and improve the efficiency and equity of pandemic response efforts.

**Keywords:** Vaccination timing; Intertemporal preferences; Behavioral medicine; COVID-19 response; Policy friction; Biological vulnerability; Institutional trust; Discrete choice experiment
