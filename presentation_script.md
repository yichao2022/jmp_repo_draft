## Section I: Introduction (Approx. 5 minutes)

### Slide 1: Title & Opening
"Good afternoon, everyone. I am Yichao Jin, a Ph.D. candidate from UT Dallas. It is a great honor to be here at the Herbert Wertheim School of Public Health to present my Job Market Paper. Today, I’ll be discussing a critical yet often overlooked dimension of health behavior: **Time**. Specifically, we use structural estimation to quantify how intertemporal preferences drive vaccination decisions."

### Slide 2: Motivation
"While existing literature heavily focuses on the 'Yes/No' of vaccine hesitancy, we shift the lens to the **'When.'** Does the waiting time to protection significantly discount the perceived value of a vaccine? Understanding this is vital for optimizing logistics, scheduling boosters, and managing future pandemic preparedness."

### Slide 3: Research Questions
"My research addresses three core questions: First, can we identify the deep structural parameters—$\delta$ and $\kappa$—that govern delayed protection? Second, is this discounting constant, or is it driven by a 'Present-Bias'? And third, how do these preferences vary across socio-economic strata and institutional trust?"

### Slide 4: Preview (The One-Slide Summary)
"Before we dive into the mechanics, here is the preview: First, we find that vaccination choices are best captured by **Hyperbolic Discounting**, indicating a strong present-bias. Second, this bias creates a psychological 'markup' of 29% on waiting time. And finally, the shadow price of a one-month delay is approximately **111 RMB**—a significant welfare barrier."

## Section II: Methodology (Approx. 10 minutes)

### Slide 5-6: Setting & Strategic Window
"Our study is set in early 2021 in Wuhan—a high risk-salience environment. Crucially, our data was collected during a **voluntary phase**, allowing us to recover 'latent' preferences before the introduction of mandates or green-code restrictions."

### Slide 7-9: Experimental Design
"We employed a Discrete Choice Experiment (DCE) using a 'COVID-like Disease' framework to minimize emotional anchoring. Respondents chose between vaccines with varying efficacy, delay to protection, and incentives. As you see on Slide 10, the core trade-off is often between **immediate protection** and **higher monetary rewards**."

### Slide 10-12: The Structural Bridge
"Our theoretical foundation is the Random Utility Framework. Unlike reduced-form studies, we explicitly map the empirical delay coefficient into structural parameters. This allows us to isolate 'Patience' from 'Present-bias' and, more importantly, it builds the bridge needed for counterfactual policy simulations."

## Section III: Results I (Approx. 12 minutes)

### Slide 13-14: The Winner (Hyperbolic vs. Exponential)
"The results are striking: The Hyperbolic model significantly outperforms the standard Exponential model. This confirms that human behavior in health protection is non-constant. We recovered a present-bias parameter, $\hat{\kappa}$, of **0.225**. This represents a 'sharp drop' in value the moment a delay is introduced."

### Slide 15-16: The BDM Reveal
"To make this intuitive, we converted $\kappa$ into the **Behavioral Delay Multiplier (BDM)**. Our estimate of 1.29 means that a 14-day biological wait for antibodies is psychologically experienced as an **18.1-day** delay. This 29% 'Psychological Markup' explains why even minor logistics delays can kill vaccine acceptance."

### Slide 17: Trust as a Stabilizer
"Who is paying this price? **Trust** is the critical moderator. For high-trust individuals, the BDM is nearly rational (1.19), but for low-trust groups, it inflates to 1.55. Trust acts as a behavioral stabilizer; without it, the perceived cost of waiting increases by over 50%."

### Slide 18-20: Validation & Robustness
"Our data quality is exceptionally high, with a **92% Dominance Pass rate**. Furthermore, the MWTA remains consistently positive and stable across all 18 demographic subgroups, proving that this time-sensitivity is a structural signal, not an outlier effect."

## Section IV: Policy Evaluation (Approx. 8 minutes)

### Slide 21: The Power of Counterfactuals
"Because we used a structural approach, we can move beyond observed data to perform **Policy Simulations**."

### Slide 22: Welfare Analysis (The 111 RMB Shadow Price)
"We first quantified the 'Shadow Price of Time.' On average, a one-month delay costs **111.4 RMB** in welfare. For vulnerable groups—the low-income and low-trust—this barrier is even steeper, representing a significant inequity in health access."

### Slide 23-24: Efficiency vs. Equity
"Our simulations show that in urban clusters, investing in **logistical velocity** (speeding up rollout) offers a much higher ROI than direct cash subsidies. However, we find that a 'one-size-fits-all' policy fails. For low-trust groups, a **hybrid approach** of subsidies and transparent communication is required to ensure equity."
