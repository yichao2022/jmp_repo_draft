\subsection{Embedding Timing Preferences in Dynamic Epidemic and Operational Models}

The current framework treats delay primarily as an individual-level welfare determinant and a driver of vaccine uptake. However, this focus potentially understates the true social cost of waiting. While the recovered MWTA captures the individual's psychological and biological burden of delayed protection, it does not fully account for the epidemiological externalities inherent in infectious disease dynamics.

Future research should aim to integrate these estimated timing preferences into dynamic transmission models (e.g., SEIR-based compartmental models) and operational queueing models. A critical gap remains between behavioral delay valuation and the broader social shadow price of delay. From a public health perspective, the ``Social Shadow Price'' of delay represents the aggregated cost of preventable infections, hospitalizations, and deaths that occur while individuals wait for protection. Currently, our Equity-Sensitive Policy Rules are derived based on individual welfare-monetary trade-offs; however, a complete social cost-benefit analysis would acknowledge that a one-month delay in a high-transmission environment generates external costs far exceeding the individual MWTA.

By combining behavioral MWTA with these epidemiological externalities, policymakers could develop a more robust foundation for timeliness investments. For instance, in the presence of high transmission (high $R_0$), the case for investing in capacity expansion (reducing the queue) becomes significantly stronger than offering monetary compensation for the delay. Integrating behavioral parameters like the Behavioral Delay Multiplier (BDM) directly into the transition rates of compartmental models would allow for a quantification of the ``Welfare-Epidemiological Gap''---the difference between what individuals are willing to accept for a delay and the actual social damage caused by that delay. Such an integrated approach would offer a more comprehensive evidence base for prioritizing operational speed-ups when outbreaks are active, shifting the policy focus from mere compensation to systemic protection.
