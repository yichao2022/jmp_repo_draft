# Abstract

**Background:** During the COVID-19 pandemic, service delays and administrative waiting times emerged as critical barriers to preventive care. However, the psychological burden of waiting—and how it is shaped by policy friction and individual vulnerability—remains under-explored in behavioral medicine.

**Method:** We conducted a discrete choice experiment (N=1,027) in Wuhan, China, to quantify waiting time as a behavioral barrier to vaccination. We utilized Mixed Logit and Latent Class models to estimate delay aversion, the marginal willingness-to-accept (MWTA) for administrative delay, and the moderating effects of institutional trust and medical vulnerability.

**Results:** Waiting time acts as a profound behavioral friction, significantly deterring uptake. On average, respondents required 47 RMB per month to offset the psychological disutility of delayed protection. This behavioral burden was highly regressive: individuals with lower institutional trust exhibited 2.2 times greater delay sensitivity than high-trust peers. Furthermore, medically vulnerable groups experienced a perceived delay burden 1.5 times higher than the general population, indicating that policy frictions disproportionately tax those at highest biological risk.

**Conclusion:** Administrative delays in COVID-19 vaccination are not merely logistical inconveniences; they function as a "behavioral time tax" that exacerbates health inequities. Mitigating policy friction and improving wait-time transparency are essential behavioral interventions to ensure rapid, equitable uptake in pandemic response and future public health crises.

**Keywords:** Vaccine uptake, Behavioral friction, Policy delay, Institutional trust, Behavioral medicine, COVID-19
