\subsection{Context Specificity and Generalizability of MWTA Estimates}

The numerical estimates of the Marginal Willingness to Accept (MWTA) for waiting time recovered in this study (approximately 111 RMB per month) are inherently tied to the specific institutional and psychological landscape of Wuhan during a period of high epidemic risk salience. To evaluate the external validity of these findings, it is essential to consider how the unique features of this environment—high centralized coordination, a specific level of institutional trust, and zero-cost vaccine delivery—interact with the behavioral parameters identified.

First, the generalizability of these findings to international health systems depends on the underlying structure of vaccine delivery and payment. In the Wuhan context, vaccination was centralized and provided free of charge, which isolated "waiting time" and "monetary incentives" as the primary policy levers. In healthcare systems characterized by high out-of-pocket costs or fragmented private insurance (e.g., the United States or several low- and middle-income countries), the behavioral cost of delay may interact significantly with financial constraints. In such settings, the sensitivity to the monetary attribute in cash-time trade-offs might be higher, which would numerically lower the recovered MWTA compared to the relatively low-cost environment of our study site.

Second, the role of institutional trust and risk perceptions is critical. As the first global epicenter of the COVID-19 pandemic, the population in Wuhan likely exhibited a heightened sensitivity to biological threats—a "salience bias" that may inflate the Shadow Price of Waiting compared to regions with less direct exposure. Furthermore, the level of institutional trust typically observed in centralized systems affects the perceived reliability of the "delivery guarantee." In more pluralistic or fragmented societies where institutional trust may be lower, citizens might perceive waiting as an indicator of service uncertainty or inequality, potentially leading to an even higher MWTA for delayed protection.

Finally, while the structural behavioral parameters—such as the presence of hyperbolic discounting ($\kappa=0.225$) and a Behavioral Delay Multiplier (BDM) greater than unity—are likely to be robust across different contexts, the numerical MWTA values and subgroup rankings are context-dependent. International comparisons (e.g., comparing our findings with the disutility of waiting in the UK's National Health Service) suggest that in high-risk health contexts, the valuation of time is driven by the speed of biological protection rather than the mere inconvenience of the wait. Therefore, applying the Equity-Sensitive Policy Rules derived in Chapter 6 to other settings would require local preference elicitation or context-specific calibration to account for differences in baseline acceptance and healthcare access structures.
